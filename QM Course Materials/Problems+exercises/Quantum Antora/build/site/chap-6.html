  2. Do the same for the square.

  3. In words, describe the symmetries of \(P_n\).

1.1.2. Problem

Show that \(\hat T(a)\hat\Pi=\hat\Pi\hat T(-a)\)

Solution

We simply compute, using the test function \(f\):

\[\begin{align*} \hat T(a)\hat\Pi f(x)&=\hat T(a) f(-x)\\ &=f(-x-a)\\ &=\hat\Pi f(x+a)\\ &=\hat\Pi \hat T(-a)f(x) \end{align*}\]

as desired.

4. Parity

4.1. Questions

4.1.1. Problem

Show that the position and momentum operators are “odd under parity”: if \(\hat\Pi\) is the parity operator, then

  1. \(\hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat x\,\hat\Pi=-\hat x\)

  2. \(\hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat p\,\hat\Pi=-\hat p\)

You may assume that \(\hat \Pi\) is hermitian.

Solution

  1. Using the test function \(f\), we have

    \[\begin{align*} \hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat x\,\hat\Pi f(x)&=\hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat x f(-x)\\ &=(-\hat x)f(x) \end{align*}\]

    So indeed \(\hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat x\,\hat\Pi=-\hat x\).

  2. Similarly,

    \[\begin{align*} \hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat p\,\hat\Pi f(x)&=\hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat p f(-x)\\ &=\hat\Pi^\dagger \left(-i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}\right)f(-x)\\ &=-i\hbar\frac{d}{d(-x)}f(x)\\ &=i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}f(x)\\ &=-\hat pf(x) \end{align*}\]

    So indeed \(\hat\Pi^\dagger \,\hat p\,\hat\Pi=-\hat p\).

4.1.2. Problem

Compute the following two operator exponentials in terms of \(\hat\Pi\), \(I\) (the identity operator), and \(e\):

  1. \(\exp(\hat\Pi)\)

  2. \(\exp(i\hat\Pi)\)

Solution

As \(\hat\Pi\) is its own inverse,

\[\begin{align*} \hat\Pi^n &=\begin{cases} \hat\Pi&n\ \textrm{odd}\\ I&n\ \textrm{even} \end{cases} \end{align*}\]

  1. Then,

    \[\begin{align*} \exp(\hat\Pi)&=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\hat\Pi^n}{n!}\\ &=\sum_{\substack{n=0\\n\ \textrm{even}}}^\infty \frac{\hat\Pi^n}{n!}+\sum_{\substack{n=1\\n\ \textrm{odd}}}^\infty \frac{\hat\Pi^n}{n!}\\ &=\sum_{\substack{n=0\\n\ \textrm{even}}}^\infty \frac{I}{n!}+\sum_{\substack{n=1\\n\ \textrm{odd}}}^\infty \frac{\hat\Pi}{n!}\\ &=I\left(\frac{e+e^{-1}}{2}\right)+\hat\Pi\left(\frac{e-e^{-1}}{2}\right)\\ &=I\cosh(1)+\hat\Pi\sinh(1) \end{align*}\]

  2. Similarly,

    \[\begin{align*} \exp(i\hat\Pi)&=\sum_{\substack{n=0\\n\ \textrm{even}}}^\infty \frac{(i\hat\Pi)^n}{n!}+\sum_{\substack{n=1\\n\ \textrm{odd}}}^\infty \frac{(i\hat\Pi)^n}{n!}\\ &=\sum_{\substack{n=0\\n\ \textrm{even}}}^\infty \frac{Ii^{n}}{n!}+\sum_{\substack{n=1\\n\ \textrm{odd}}}^\infty \frac{\hat\Pi i^{n}}{n!}\\ &=I\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{i^{2n}}{(2n)!}+\hat\Pi\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{i^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\\ &=I\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n}}{(2n)!}+i\hat\Pi\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n}}{(2n+1)!}\\ &=I\cos(1)+\hat\Pi\ i\sin(1) \end{align*}\]

8. Translations in Time

8.1. Questions

8.1.1. Problem

The Generalized Ehrenfest Theorem states that

\[\frac{d}{dt}\braket{\hat Q}=\frac{1}{i\hbar}\Braket{[\hat H, \hat Q]}+\Braket{\frac{\partial \hat Q}{\partial t}}\]

In particular, if \(\hat Q\) commutes with the Hamiltonian, then the time-derivative of the expectation of \(\hat Q\) is the expectation of the time-derivative of \(\hat Q\).

But, in the Schrödinger picture, operators remain constant, while in the Heisenberg picture they change over time. So in the Schrödinger picture, the RHS of this equation is zero, while in the Heisenberg picture it’s nonzero. But in both cases the LHS of the equation is the same (and it’s an observable quantity, so it had better not depend on the formulation of QM used).

Reconcile this with the Generalized Ehrenfest Theorem. Where in particular in the proof of the Theorem does the issue lie?

8.1.2. Problem

In the quantum harmonic oscillator, where \(\hat H=\frac{1}{2m}\hat p^2+\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2\hat x^2\), let \(\hat x_H(t)\) and \(\hat p_H(t)\) be the Heisenberg picture position and momentum operators. Show that \(\frac{d}{dt}\hat x_H(t)=\frac{1}{m\omega^2}\hat p_H(t)\) and \(\frac{d}{dt}\hat p_H(t)=-m\omega^2\hat x_H(t)\) — just as is the case in the classical situation.
