  • .. Why is \(\Psi_E(x,t)= \psi_E(x)e^{-iEt/\hbar}\) called a stationary state if it is time-dependent?

    1. Show that \(|\Psi_E(x,t)|^2 = |\psi_E(x)|^2\).

    2. Show that \( \Braket{p} = 0\) for \(\Psi_E(x,t)\).

    Solution

    1.   \[\begin{align*}| \Psi_E(x,t)|^2 &= \Psi_E^* \Psi_E\\ &= (\psi_E^*(x)e^{iEt/\hbar}) (\psi_E(x) e^{-iEt/\hbar}) \\ &= |\psi_E(x)|^2 \end{align*}\]

    2. For a particle in state \(\Psi_E\), \[\begin{align*} \Braket{x}&=\int_{\mathcal D} x|\Psi_E(x,t)|^2 \,dx\\ &=\int_{\mathcal D} x|\psi_E(x)|^2\,dx\\ &=\textrm{constant w.r.t time} \end{align*}\] So \(\frac{d\Braket{x}}{dt}=0\). Then, Ehrenfest’s theorem says that \(\Braket{p}=m\frac{d\Braket{x}}{dt}=0\).

  • .. If you were to measure the energy of a particle with the wavefunction \(\Psi(x,t) = \frac{4}{5}\Psi_1(x,t) - \frac{3i}{5} \Psi_2(x,t)\), what is the probability that you‚Äôd measure the energy to be \(E_2\)?

    Solution

    When a wavefunction is written as \(\Psi(x,t)=\sum_n c_n\Psi_n(x)=\sum_n c_n\psi_n(x) e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}\), the probability that a measurement of the energy yields \(E_n\) is \(|c_n|^2\). The probability that energy is \(E_2\), then, is \(\left|c_2\right|^2=\left|-\frac{3i}{5}\right|^2=\frac{9}{25}\). (As there are only two eigenstates in this example, the probability of measuring an energy of \(E_1\) must be one minus this, or \(\frac{16}{25}\).)

2. The Infinite Square Well

2.1. Questions

  • .. The solutions to the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation in the infinite square well of width \(a\) are \(\psi_n(x)=\sqrt{2/a}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\) with \(n\ge 1\). Show that these functions are orthonormal, i.e., that \(\Braket{\psi_m,\psi_n}\) is 1 when \(m=n\) and 0 otherwise. (Hint: \(\cos\) is an even function, \(\sin\) is an odd function, and \(\cos(a+b)=\cos a\cos b-\sin a\sin b\).)

    Solution

    Using the trigonometric identities in the hint, we have \[\begin{align*} \cos(a+b)&=\cos a\cos b-\sin a\sin b\\ \cos(a-b)&=\cos a\cos b+\sin a\sin b\\ \cos(a+b)-\cos(a-b)&=-2\sin a\sin b\\ \sin a \sin b &=\frac{1}{2}(\cos(a-b) - \cos(a+b)) \end{align*}\] Regardless of whether \(m=n\), \[\begin{align*} \Braket{\psi_m,\psi_n}&= \int_0^a \psi_m^*\psi_n\,dx\\ &=\int_0^a \sqrt\frac{2}{a}\sin\left(\frac{m\pi}{a}x\right)\ \sqrt\frac{2}{a}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx\\ &=\frac{1}{a}\int_0^a \cos\left(\frac{(m-n)\pi}{a}x\right)-\cos\left(\frac{(m+n)\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx \end{align*}\] And for \(k\ne 0\), \[\begin{align*} \int_0^a \cos\left(\frac{k\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx=\left.\frac{a}{k\pi}\sin\left(\frac{k\pi}{a}x\right)\right|^a_0=0 \end{align*}\] When \(m=n\), \(\Braket{\psi_m,\psi_n}\) becomes \[\begin{align*} \frac{1}{a}\int_0^a \cos 0-\cos\left(\frac{(m+n)\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx=\frac{1}{a}\int_0^a dx=1 \end{align*}\] When \(m\ne n\), both of the \(\cos\) terms in the integral are of the form \(\cos\left(\frac{k\pi}{a}x\right),k\ne 0\) and so they both integrate to 0, and then \(\Braket{\psi_m,\psi_n}=0\).

  • .. Let \(V\) be the potential in the infinite square well: \[V(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \leq x \leq a \\ \infty & \textrm{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

    1. Find the general solution using the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation, the simple harmonic oscillator equation, and applying the constraints of the infinite square well.

    2. Apply the boundary conditions to solve for the constants \(A\) and \(B\).

    3. Find solutions for \(kx\) and, using those results, solve for the possible values of the energy \(E\).

    4. How does this result compare to what we‚Äôd expect from a classical case?

    5. Use this result and normalize \(\Psi\) to solve for stationary states.

    6. What is the significance of this result?

    Solution

    1. Inside the well, \(V(x) = 0\), and so the Schr√∂dinger equation says \(\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2}=E\psi(x)\). Letting \(k=\sqrt{2mE/\hbar}\), we have \(\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2}= -k^2\psi(x)\). Assuming \(E\geq0\), the solution isn‚Äôt contradicting the boundary conditions. Recognize: \(\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + k^2\psi = 0\) as the equation of the simple harmonic oscillator. The solution of this equation is \(\psi(x)=A\sin(kx)+B\cos(kx)\) where \(A,B\) are constants determined by the boundary conditions.

    2. For \(\psi\) to be continuous at \(0\) and \(a\), we must have \(\psi(0) =\psi(a)=0\). So \(\psi(0) = A\sin(0) + B\cos(0) = B\), and hence \(B=0\). This leaves us with: \(\psi(x)=A\sin(kx)\), so either \(A=0\) ‚Äî in which case \(\psi\) is 0 everywhere, which is not a valid wavefunction ‚Äî or \(\sin(ka)=0\).

    3. The boundary condition \(\psi(a)=A\sin(ka)=0\) requires that \(k= \frac{n\pi}{a}\) with \(n\) an integer. But if \(n=0\) then \(\psi(x)=0\) everywhere, so \(n\) must in fact be nonzero. Additionally, \(\sin\left(\frac{-n\pi}{a}x\right)=-\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\) ‚Äî a solution we’ve already accounted for, since \(A\) can absorb the leading minus sign ‚Äî so the distinct solutions are \(k= \frac{n\pi}{a}\) for positive integer \(n\). Therefore the permissible values of the energy \(E\) are \(E_n=\frac{\hbar^2 k_n^2}{2m} =\frac{\pi^2 n^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}\) with \(n\) a positive integer.

    4. Unlike the classical case, a quantum particle in the infinite square well cannot have an arbitrary value of energy. Instead, a quantum particle can only be in a superposition of one of these special allowed states that fit within the constraints of the infinite square well. When observed, a quantum particle in the infinite square well will have an energy that is one of the \(E_n\).

    5. To compute the integral, we’ll need the fact that \(\sin^2(a)=\frac{1}{2}(1-\cos(2a))\). \[\begin{align*} |\psi_n(x)|^2&=\int_0^a |A|^2\sin^2\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx\\ &=|A|^2\int_0^a\frac{1}{2}\left[1-\cos\left(\frac{2n\pi}{a}x\right)\right]\,dx\\ &=\frac{|A|^2}{2}\left.\left[x-\frac{\sin(\frac{2n\pi}{a}x)}{\frac{2n\pi}{a}}\right]\right|^a_0\\ &=\frac{|A|^2a}{2} \end{align*}\] For the norm to be 1, we must have \(|A|=\sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\). Any choice of \(A\) satisfying this will do ‚Äî \(A\) can be complex, but its phase doesn’t matter ‚Äî so let’s pick the most obvious and simplest choice, \(A=\sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\), to obtain \(\psi_n(x)=\sqrt\frac{2}{a}\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{a}x\right)\). (In general, it’s always simplest to choose the stationary states that are real-valued.)

  • .. What are the four properties of the functions \(\Psi_n(x)\)? Are these properties unique to the infinite square well? Why are they so important?

    Solution

    1. These functions are alternatively even and odd about the center of the potential well. This is true for any symmetrical potential well.

    2. With increasing \(n\), each successive state has one more node in the wavefunction. This is true regardless of the shape of the potential well.

    3. They are mutually orthogonal: \(\int \psi_m(x)^*\psi_n(x)\,dx=\delta_{mn}\). This is true regardless of the potential well, as long as it’s normalized.

    4. The state functions form a complete set. This is not universal, but is almost always applicable for any potential we will encounter.

    These properties are so important because they hold true for almost all potentials, which means we can apply these four properties to help solve many different types of problems.

  • .. A particle in the infinite square well of width \(a\) has the following initial wave function: \(\Psi(x,0)=A(\psi_1(x) + \psi_4(x))\).

    1. Normalize to find \(A\). (You can assume \(A\) is real and positive.)

    2. Find the constants \(c_n\) in the expression \(\Psi(x,0)=\sum_n c_n\psi_n(x)\).

    3. Compute \(\left|\Psi(x,t)\right|^2\) (this isn’t very useful on its own, but you’ll need it for part (d)).

    4. Compute \(\langle x\rangle\) as a function of \(t\). What is the frequency of oscillations? What is the amplitude?

    Solution

    1. To normalize, we set \(\left\|\Psi\right\|^2=\int_0^a\left|\Psi(x,0)\right|^2\,dx=1\) and simply compute, using the fact that the \(\psi_n\) are orthonormal over \([0,a]\). (In the integrals below, for brevity, we omit the \(x\) as an argument of the \(\psi_n\).) \[\begin{align*} 1&=\int_0^a\left|\Psi(x,0)\right|\\ &=\int_0^a\left|A(\psi_1+\psi_4)\right|^2\,dx\\ &=\int_0^a\left|A\right|^2(\psi_1^2+2\psi_1\psi_4+\psi_4^2)\,dx\\ &=\left|A\right|^2(1+2\cdot 0+1)\\ &=2\left|A\right|^2 \end{align*}\] So \(A=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) (or any complex number with that magnitude, but we can always take the normalization constant to be real, so we might as well do so.)

    2. Simply referring to the expression for \(\Psi\) in the problem statement, we have \[\Psi(x,0)=A(\psi_1(x)+\psi_4(x))=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\psi_1(x)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\psi_4(x)\] so \(c_1=c_4=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) and the other \(c_n\) are zero.

    3. In general, if \(\Psi(x,0)=\sum_n c_n\psi_n(x)\), then \(\Psi(x,t)=\sum_n c_n\psi_n(x)\varphi_n(t) \) where \(\varphi_n(t):=e^{-iE_nt/\hbar}\). And if we write \(E_n=\frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}=n^2E_1\), then \(\varphi_n(t)=e^{-i n^2E_1t/\hbar}\), and so we can compute something that we’ll need very soon: \[\begin{align*} \varphi_1(t)\varphi_4^* (t) &= e^{-i E_1t/\hbar}\left(e^{-16i E_1t/\hbar}\right)^* =e^{15iE_1t/\hbar} \end{align*}\] which means \[\begin{align*} \varphi_1(t)\varphi_4^* (t) + \varphi_1^*(t)\varphi_4(t)&=\varphi_1(t)\varphi_4^* (t) + (\varphi_1(t)\varphi_4^*(t))^*\\ &=2\Re[\varphi_1(t)\varphi_4^*(t)]\\ &=2\cos(15E_1t/\hbar) \end{align*}\]

      Now to compute \(\left|\Psi(x,t)\right|^2\). Below, for brevity, we’ll write \(\varphi_n\) instead of \(\varphi_n(t)\) and \(\psi_n\) instead of \(\psi_n(x)\), and use the fact that \(c_n\) and \(\psi_n\) are real. \[\begin{align*} \left|\Psi(x,t)\right|^2&=\Psi(x,t)^*\Psi(x,t)\\ &=\left(c_1\psi_1\varphi_1+c_4\psi_4\varphi_4\right)^*\left(c_1\psi_1\varphi_1+c_4\psi_4\varphi_4\right)\\ &=\left|c_1\psi_1\varphi_1\right|^2+\left|c_4\psi_4\varphi_4\right|^2\\ &\quad+((c_1\varphi_1)^* (c_4\varphi_4)+(c_1\varphi_1)(c_4\varphi_4)^*)\psi_1\psi_4\\ &=c_1^2\psi_1^2+c_4^2\psi_4^2+c_1c_4(\varphi_1^* \varphi_4+\varphi_1\varphi_4^*)\psi_1\psi_4\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\psi_1^2+\frac{1}{2}\psi_4^2+\cos(15E_1t/\hbar)\psi_1\psi_4 \end{align*}\]

    4. We wish to compute \(\braket x\) (as a function of \(t\)), which is just the following expression: \[\begin{align*} \braket x&=\int_0^a x\left|\Psi(x,t)\right|^2\,dx \end{align*}\] Before proceeding we’ll want the following facts (which you can feel free to derive on your own). If \(m\) and \(n\) are integers, then if \(m\ne n\), then \[\begin{align*} \int_0^a x\sin\left(\frac{m\pi}{a}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx&=\frac{2mna^2((-1)^{m+n}-1)}{(m^2-n^2)^2\pi^2} \end{align*}\] and if \(m=n\) then \[\begin{align*} \int_0^a x\sin\left(\frac{m\pi}{a}x\right)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx=\int_0^a x\sin^2\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}x\right)\,dx=\frac{a^2}{4} \end{align*}\] (Note that this second one does not actually depend on \(n\).) So, \[\begin{align*} \int_0^a x\,\psi_1^2\,dx&=\int_0^a x\left[\sqrt{2/a}\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{a} x\right)\right]^2\,dx\\ &=\frac{2}{a}\int_0^ax\sin^2\left(\frac{\pi}{a} x\right)\,dx\\ &=\frac{2}{a}\cdot\frac{a^2}{4}\\ &=\frac{a}{2}\\\\ \int_0^a x\,\psi_4^2\,dx&=\frac{a}{2}\\\\ \int_0^a x\,\psi_1\psi_4 \,dx&=\int_0^ax\left[\sqrt{2/a}\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{a}x\right)\right]\left[\sqrt{2/a}\sin\left(\frac{4\pi}{a}x\right)\right]\,dx\\ &=\frac{2}{a}\cdot\frac{2\cdot1\cdot4a^2(-2)}{(1^2-4^2)^2\pi^2}\\ &=-\frac{32a}{225\pi^2} \end{align*}\]

      So, we may finally compute our answer: \[\begin{align*} \braket x&=\int_0^a x\left|\Psi(x,t)\right|^2\,dx\\ &=\int_0^a x\left(\frac{1}{2}\psi_1^2+\frac{1}{2}\psi_4^2+\cos(15E_1t/\hbar)\psi_1\psi_4\right)\,dx\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a}{2}\right)+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a}{2}\right)+\cos(15E_1t/\hbar)\left(-\frac{32a}{225\pi^2}\right)\\ &=\frac{a}{2}-\frac{32a\cos(15E_1t/\hbar)}{225\pi^2}\\ &=a\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{32}{225\pi^2}\cos(15E_1t/\hbar)\right) \end{align*}\] The frequency of oscillation is \(\frac{15E_1}{2\pi\hbar}=\frac{15\pi\hbar}{4ma^2}\). The particle oscillates between \(a\left(\frac{1}{2}\pm\frac{32}{225\pi^2}\right)\) and so its amplitude is \(\frac{64a}{225\pi^2}\).

      Notably, unlike a classical particle, its amplitude is not \(\frac{a}{2}\) (\(\frac{64}{225\pi^2}\approx0.0288\ll 0.5\)). The expected position of a quantum mechanical particle cannot get arbitrarily close to the walls of the well ‚Äî why not?

3. The Harmonic Oscillator

3.1. Questions

  • .. Show that every solution of the time-independent harmonic oscillator Schr√∂dinger equation \(\hat H\psi = E\psi\) lies on the same “ladder”: for every pair of solutions \(\psi_a,\psi_b\) with \(a >b\), there is some \(m\) for which \(\psi_a=k\hat a_{+}^m\psi_b\) (for some normalization constant \(k\)).

    Solution

    Suppose \(\psi_a\) lies on ladder \(A\) and \(\psi_b\) on ladder \(B\). By successively applying the lowering operator to \(\psi_a\) and \(\psi_b\), we obtain the bottommost rungs on the two ladders, which we’ll denote \(\psi_{A,0}\) and \(\psi_{B,0}\). By virtue of being the bottommost rungs, they must satisfy \(\hat a_-\psi_{A,0}=\hat a_-\psi_{B,0}=0\). Substituting \(\hat a_-=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar m\omega}}(i\hat p+m\omega x)\), we obtain the differential equation \(\frac{d\psi_{L,0}}{dx}=-\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}x\psi_{L,0}\) for \(L=A,B\). In both cases this leads to the same solution: \[ \psi_{A,0}(x)=\psi_{B,0}(x)=\left(\frac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{\frac{1}{4}}e^{-\frac{m\omega}{2\hbar}x^2} \] So the ladders share their bottom rung, which we may now write as just \(\psi_0(x)\). Every subsequent rung ‚Äî on either ladder ‚Äî is given by successive applications of the raising operator: \(\psi_{A,n}=\psi_{B,n}=k(\hat a_+)^n\psi_{0}\). So, \[\begin{align*} \psi_a&=k(\hat a_+)^{a}\psi_0\\ &=k(\hat a_+)^{a-b}(\hat a_+)^b\psi_0\\ &=k(\hat a_+)^{n-b}\psi_b \end{align*}\] as desired.

5. The Delta-Function Potential

5.1. Questions

  • .. For a fixed constant \(a\), compute the closed form for \(f(x)\coloneqq\int_{-\infty}^x\delta (t-a)\,dt\).

    Solution

    First, use \(u\)-substitution with \(u=t-a\) to obtain \(f(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x-a} \delta(u)\,du\). Then, if \(x< a\), the integrand is everywhere 0, so \(f(x)=0\). Meanwhile if \(x> a\), then the integrand includes \(\delta(0)\) and so \(f(x)=1\). The value of \(f(a)\) depends on your interpretation of \(\int_{-\infty}^0\delta(t)\,dt\), which could be undefined, \(\frac{1}{2}\), or 1. So we have \[\begin{align*} f(x)&=\begin{cases} 0&x<a\\ \textrm{?}&x=a\\ 1&x>a \end{cases} \end{align*}\]

    Why is it so hard to define \(\int_{-\infty}^0\delta (t)\,dt\)? There are three possible values:

    • Undefined, simply because by definition the only integrals of the \(\delta\) function that exist are those containing an interval containing \(0\).

    • \(\frac{1}{2}\), because \(\delta\) is an even “function”, so its integral from \(-\infty\) to \(0\) should be half of its integral from \(-\infty\) to \(\infty\).

    • \(1\), because the domain of integration includes everywhere that \(\delta(x)\ne 0\).

    This helps to illustrate the thorniness of \(\delta\) and why it is not considered a function at all.

  • .. Show that the \(\delta\) function can be approximated by a \(0\)-centered Gaussian \(\varphi(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{x^2}{\sigma^2}}\) whose variance \(\sigma^2\) approaches 0.

    Solution

    Clearly, the integral of a Gaussian over the number line is 1, no matter what. As its variance approaches 0, its integral over domains that do not include the origin must approach 0, i.e., for \(\varepsilon>0\), \[\begin{align*} \lim_{\sigma^2\to0}\int_{-\infty}^{-\varepsilon}\mathcal \varphi(x)\,dx=\lim_{\sigma^2\to0}\int_{\varepsilon}^{\infty}\mathcal \varphi(x)\,dx=0 \end{align*}\] Since \(\varphi(x)>0\) for all \(x\), the only way the above integrals approach \(0\) as \(\sigma^2\to 0\) is if \(\varphi(x)\to 0\) for \(x\ne 0\). Since \(\varphi(x)\to0\) when \(x\ne 0\) and \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty\varphi(x)\,dx=1\), it is indeed that case that \(\lim_{\sigma^2\to 0}\varphi(x)=\delta(x)\).

  • .. Show the following: \[\displaystyle\delta(x)=\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kx}}\right)\] This provides another continuous approximation of the \(\delta\) function which improves as \(k\) grows.

    Solution

    The simplest way to show this is to observe how the expression on the RHS behaves when integrated: \[\begin{align*} &\phantom{=}\int_{-\infty}^z \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kx}}\right)\,dx\\ &= \lim_{k\to\infty}\int_{-\infty}^z\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kx}}\right)\,dx\\ &= \lim_{k\to\infty}\left(\left.\frac{1}{1+e^{-kx}}\right|_{-\infty}^z\right)\\ &= \lim_{k\to\infty}\left(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kz}}-0\right) \end{align*}\] When \(z< 0\), \(e^{-kz}\to\infty\) and so \(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kz}}\to0\), making the integral 0. Meanwhile, when \(z>0\), \(e^{-kz}\to0\) and so \(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kz}}\to1\), making the integral 1. So, \[\begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^z \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{1+e^{-kx}}\right)\,dx&=\begin{cases} 0&z<0\\ 1&z>0 \end{cases} \end{align*}\] which does indeed match the \(\delta\) function.

6. The Finite Square Well

6.1. Questions

  • .. When is the square well approximation valid and why is it usable to make fair predictions?

    Solution

    If the de Broglie wavelength of the particle (\(\lambda = \hbar/p\)) \(<\) \(\Delta x_1\) \(\Delta x_2\), the square well approximation is valid. (Unless the particle is relativistic). Nothing we are trying to predict, like transmission and reflection coefficients, changes after making this approximation (as long as the above condition is met).

  • .. Find the general solution to the finite square well differential equations for each section of the well (with \(V_0>0\)): \[V(x) = \begin{cases} -V_0 & |x| \le a \\ 0 & |x| > a \end{cases} \]

    1. Left side of the well (\(x<-a\))

    2. Middle of the well (\(-a\le x\le a\))

    3. Right side of the well (\(x>a\))

    Solution

    1. If \(V(x) = 0\) then the Schr√∂dinger equation says \(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx} = E\psi\). Then \(\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}=k^2\psi\) where \(k=-\sqrt{2mE/\hbar}>0\) (because \(E< 0\)), which has solutions of the form \(\psi_1(x)=Ae^{-kx} + Be^{kx}\).

    2. If \(V(x) = -V_0\) then Schr√∂dinger equation says \(\left(-V_0-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx}\right) \psi= E\psi\), so \(\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}=-\frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E+V_0)\psi\), which has solutions \(\psi_2(x)=C\sin(lx)+D\cos(lx)\) where \(l=\sqrt{2m(E+V_0)} > 0\).

    3. This is the same as part (a): \(\psi_3(x) = Ee^{-kx}+Fe^{kx}\).

  • .. Using your results from 2.6.2, apply the boundary conditions to all the general solutions. Use these equations and their coefficients to derive the transcendental equation: \((k-il)/(k+il)= \pm e^{2ila}\). (Hint: \(k\) as coefficient from \(a\) and \(l\) as coefficient from \(b\).)

    Solution

    The boundary conditions constitute the following:

    1. Finite as \(x\to-\infty\): \(\psi_1(x\rightarrow-\infty) < \infty\).

    2. Finite as \(x\to-\infty\): \(\psi_3(x\rightarrow\infty) < \infty\).

    3. Continuous at the boundaries: \(\psi_1(-a)=\psi_2(-a)\) and \(\psi_2(a) = \psi_3(a)\).

    4. The derivative of the wave function is continuous at boundaries: \[\begin{align*} \left.\frac{d\psi_1}{dx}\right|_{x=-a} &= \left.\frac{d\psi_2}{dx}\right|_{x=-a}\\ \left.\frac{d\psi_2}{dx}\right|_{x=a} &= \left.\frac{d\psi_3}{dx}\right|_{x=a} \end{align*}\]

    Applying these conditions to the results from 2.6.2:

    1. \(\psi_1(-\infty)<\infty\) implies that \(A=0\) and so \(\psi_1(x)=Be^{kx}\).

    2. \(\psi_3(\infty)<\infty\) implies that \(D=0\) and so \(\psi_3(x)=Ce^{-kx}\).

    3. As in part (b) of 2.6.2, let \(\psi_2(x)=Ce\) \(\frac{}{} \psi_1(x=-\infty)<\infty\) A=0 \(\psi_1(x) = Be^{kx}\)

    2. \(\psi_3(x=\infty)<\infty\) D=0 \(\psi_3(x)=Ce^{-kx}\)

    3. Let \(\psi_2 = Ce^{ilx} + De^{-ilx}\) \(Be^{-ka}=Ce^{-ila} + De^{ila}\)(eq.1) and \(Be^{-ka} = Cile^{-ila} - Dile^{ila}\)(eq.2) Now take: k(eq.1) - (eq.2): \(Cke^{-ila} + Dke^{ila} = Cile^{-ila} - Dile^{ila}\) \(C(ile^{-ila} - ke^{-ila}) = D(ke^{-ila} + ile^{ila})\) \(C/D = - ((k+il)/(k-il))e^{2ila}\) (eq.*)

    4. \(\psi_2(x=a)=\psi_3(x=a) an\frac{d d\psi_2}{dx}|_{x=a} = \frac{d\psi_3}{dx}|_{x=a}\) \(Ce^{-ila} + De^{-ila} = Ee^{-ka}\)(eq.3) and \(ilCe^{-ila}-ilDe^{-ila}=-kEe^{-ka}\)(eq.4)

    Now take: k(eq.3) + (eq.4):

    \(Cke^{-ila}+Dke^{-ila}+ilCe^{-ila}-ilDe^{-ila}=0\)

    \(C(ke^{-ila}+ile^{-ila})=-D(ke^{-ila}-ile^{-ila})\)

    \(C/D = - ((k+il)/(k-il))e^{-2ila}\) (eq.**)

    Now set (eq.* = eq. **): \(((k+il)/(k-il))e^{2ila}=((k-il)/(k+il))e^{-2ila}\) \(((k-il)/(k+il))^2=e^{4ila}\) \((k-il)/(k+il) = \pm e^{2ila}\)
